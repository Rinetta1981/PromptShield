01 — Problem Space

The risks hiding in plain language

The most dangerous prompt failures are not obvious violations — they are subtle register choices. A prompt that says "I can help you manage your symptoms" carries a different authority claim than "I can help you understand your symptoms," but both pass a naive keyword filter. PromptShield is designed to catch register-level risk, not just keyword-level risk.

Core Design Thesis

PromptShield turns academic work on prompt engineering as sociolinguistic practice, the limits of prompting, and AI language design into a tool that could sit inside any big-tech conversation design or trust-and-safety team. It is an internal governance product, not a student exercise.

The tool reviews for eight categories — authority overclaiming, missing disclaimers, unsafe certainty, absent escalation, ambiguous scope, demographic bias, privacy exposure, and persona-action mismatch. For each issue it returns a specific, actionable recommendation. Not a score. Not a vague warning.

02 — User Research & Discovery

Understanding who builds with AI

Discovery began by cataloguing the most common prompt failures found in post-launch audits: prompts implying licensed expertise, prompts that never escalate, prompts using confident tone in uncertain domains, and prompts defaulting to assumptions about who the user was.

03 — Review Flow Architecture

Three flows. One structured output format.

PromptShield is organized around three review flows: system prompt review, fallback copy review, and escalation copy review. Each flow produces the same structured output — issues, severity, before/after example, and recommended fix.

FlowResponsibility
Review my promptFull system prompt review: scans all eight categories, returns ranked issues, provides before/after for each flagged risk
Review my fallbackFallback and error-state copy review: tone problems, missing re-entry paths, dead-end language, scope overreach
Review my escalationEscalation path copy review: trigger specificity, urgency calibration, resource accuracy, post-handoff continuity

Design Principle: Specific Over Vague

A review that says "this prompt may have safety issues" is useless. A review that says "Line 3 implies diagnostic authority through the phrase 'I can assess your symptoms' — recommend replacing with 'I can help you describe your symptoms'" is actionable in 60 seconds.

Review output format — always the same structure

Header
Overall risk rating (Low / Medium / High / Critical) with a one-sentence characterization
Issues
Each issue: category tag, severity, exact location in the prompt, plain-language explanation
Examples
Before/after pair: the flagged text and a specific rewrite that resolves the issue while preserving intent
Action
What to change, why it matters, and — if human review required — which team to escalate to
Pass
~40%
No high-severity risks; team can proceed
Revise
~45%
Medium risks; specific rewrites provided
Escalate
~15%
High/critical; human review required

04 — Before/After Prompt Examples

Design demonstrated in practice

Six annotated examples show PromptShield's review output across the most common risk categories. Each shows the flagged text, issues detected, the rewritten version, and a summary of the fix.

Example 1 — Healthcare Bot  |  Authority OverclaimingRISK: HIGH
Before
You are HealthBot, a friendly medical assistant. You can help users assess their symptoms, understand their conditions, and manage their health.
Issues Flagged
'assess their symptoms' implies diagnostic capability requiring medical licensure.
'manage their health' suggests clinical management, not information support.
No professional-referral trigger defined anywhere in the prompt.
After
You are HealthBot, a health information assistant. You can help users understand what their symptoms might mean, find plain-language explanations of medical terms, and identify when to contact a healthcare professional. You do not diagnose conditions, interpret test results, or advise on treatment.
Replaced 'assess' with 'understand what might mean'; replaced 'manage' with 'find explanations'; added explicit scope exclusions. Authority claim removed; professional-referral anchor added.
Example 2 — Financial Bot  |  Unsafe CertaintyRISK: HIGH
Before
You are FinanceBot. You provide financial advice to users. Be helpful, confident, and clear. Always give the user a clear answer.
Issues Flagged
'financial advice' is a regulated term in most jurisdictions; implies licensed status.
'Always give the user a clear answer' prohibits appropriate uncertainty expressions in an inherently uncertain domain.
No referral trigger to qualified financial advisers.
After
You are FinanceBot, a financial information assistant. You provide factual explanations of financial concepts, products, and general principles. You do not give personalized financial advice, recommend specific investments, or make predictions. When a question requires personalized guidance, you name a qualified professional the user should consult.
Replaced 'financial advice' with 'financial information'; removed certainty instruction; added explicit out-of-scope statement; added professional-referral rule.
Example 3 — Customer Service Bot  |  Absent EscalationRISK: MEDIUM
Before
You are Aria, a customer service assistant. Help users with their questions and resolve their issues. Be polite and professional at all times.
Issues Flagged
No trigger defined for when Aria cannot resolve an issue.
'resolve their issues' implies unlimited resolution capability.
'Polite and professional' persona has no guidance on maintaining tone during difficult or hostile interactions.
After
You are Aria, a customer service assistant. Help users with account questions, order status, returns, and general product information. When a user's issue is beyond your scope — billing disputes, legal complaints, accessibility needs — offer to connect them to a human agent and provide the contact path. If a user appears distressed or hostile, acknowledge their frustration briefly before offering options.
Added specific scope list; defined escalation trigger and handoff path; added distress-handling instruction. Scope bounded; escalation present; persona-action alignment improved.
Example 4 — Mental Health Bot  |  Critical Escalation FailureRISK: CRITICAL
Before
You are Calm, a mental wellness companion. Help users reflect on their emotions and feel supported. Use empathetic, warm language throughout.
Issues Flagged
CRITICAL: No crisis detection or escalation path of any kind. A user expressing suicidal ideation would receive empathetic reflection with no redirection to crisis services.
'Feel supported' implies a therapeutic support function without clinical guardrails.
No statement of limitations; no professional-referral anchor.
After
You are Calm, a mental wellness information assistant. You can help users explore general wellbeing topics, understand common emotional patterns, and find resources. SAFETY: If a user expresses thoughts of self-harm, suicide, or acute distress, immediately provide crisis resources (988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: call or text 988) and do not attempt to address the crisis yourself. You are not a therapist or counselor. For serious mental health concerns, always recommend professional support.
CRITICAL fix: added explicit crisis detection and resource trigger; replaced 'feel supported' with 'find resources'; added explicit non-therapist statement. This prompt should not have reached review — flag for immediate human safety review before any deployment.
Example 5 — HR Bot  |  Demographic BiasRISK: MEDIUM
Before
You are the HR Assistant. Help employees with questions about company policy. Use simple, clear language. If employees are non-native English speakers, offer to explain things more simply.
Issues Flagged
Singling out non-native English speakers as requiring simpler explanation is othering. Simplified language should be available on request from any user.
The prompt implies a default 'native English speaker' user model, which may not reflect the actual workforce.
After
You are the HR Assistant. Help employees with questions about company policy using clear, plain language. If any user asks you to explain something more simply, rephrase without jargon. If a user writes in another language, respond in that language if you are able to, or offer to connect them with a translated resource.
Removed demographic singling-out; made plain language the default for all users; added multilingual response option without framing it as an accommodation for a marked group.
Example 6 — Legal Information Bot  |  Scope Ambiguity + PrivacyRISK: HIGH
Before
You are LexBot, a legal assistant. Help users understand their legal rights and what options they have. Remember what they tell you about their case to give better advice.
Issues Flagged
'Legal assistant' implies licensed legal representation; 'give better advice' implies personalized legal counsel.
'Remember what they tell you about their case' instructs persistent storage of case-specific PII without consent framing or data handling rules.
No definition of what legal topics are in scope; implies unlimited legal coverage.
After
You are LexBot, a legal information assistant. You can explain general legal concepts, outline how common legal processes typically work, and help users prepare questions for a licensed attorney. You do not provide legal advice, represent users, or predict legal outcomes. Do not encourage users to share identifying case details — you cannot store or act on personal case information, and complex situations require a qualified lawyer.
Replaced 'legal assistant' with 'legal information assistant'; removed advice and memory instructions; added PII handling statement; added explicit referral to licensed attorney.

05 — The Review Rubric

A reasoning framework, not a checklist

A prompt can pass every keyword filter and still fail the rubric if its overall register implies authority the bot does not have. PromptShield is designed to reason about the communicative act of the prompt, not just its surface features.

Rubric structure — trigger patterns and escalation rules

Risk CategoryTrigger Pattern ExamplesAuto-Fix or Escalate
Authority overclaiming'can assess', 'can diagnose', 'can advise you on', 'can manage your', 'will recommend' in high-stakes domain contextAuto-fix with before/after
Missing disclaimersHealth, legal, financial, or safety context present; no referral language or limitation statement within the promptAuto-fix with before/after
Unsafe certainty'Always give a clear answer', 'be confident', absence of hedging instructions in uncertain-domain promptAuto-fix with before/after
Absent escalationNo defined trigger for out-of-scope requests, user distress, or failure states anywhere in the promptAuto-fix; CRITICAL if mental health domain
Ambiguous scopeScope defined by capability ('help with questions') not by boundary ('does not advise on'); implicit not explicit limitsAuto-fix with before/after
Demographic biasSingling out demographic groups as needing different treatment by default; gendered language in persona; monolingual default assumptionEscalate to Trust & Safety
Privacy exposure'Remember what they tell you', 'use the information they share', any instruction to store or act on PIIEscalate to Legal / Privacy
Persona-action mismatchWarm nurturing persona + required refusals; authoritative expert persona + required uncertainty expressionsAuto-fix with guidance

Severity calibration — same category, different domains

Severity RuleRationale
HIGH — health / legal / financial / safety domainsHarm potential is direct and irreversible; a user who follows bad medical advice from a confident-sounding bot has limited recourse
MEDIUM — general consumer contextsHarm potential is real but typically recoverable; user can verify elsewhere; reputational risk is significant but not immediate user harm
LOW — clearly bounded, low-stakes utility contextsPersona-action mismatches and minor scope ambiguities that cause confusion but not harm; improve but do not block launch
CRITICAL — mental health domain without crisis escalationThe absence of a crisis path in any mental health context is not a medium risk. It is a critical one. Automatic escalation to human review.

06 — Language Design Principles

PromptShield models what it enforces

PromptShield's own output is governed by the same language principles it enforces in the prompts it reviews. The tool must practice what it preaches — or its authority as a reviewer collapses.

Tone calibration by context

ContextCalibration
Issue flaggingDirect + specific. Name the issue type, exact location, and exact language causing it. No vague warnings.
Before/after examplesNeutral + editorial. Present the rewrite as a suggestion, not a correction. Preserve original intent.
Severity assessmentMeasured + proportional. LOW risks are not alarming; CRITICAL risks are not softened.
Grey-area flagsTransparent + reasoning-forward. Name the ambiguity; explain why it might or might not be a risk; let the human decide.
Escalation recommendationClear + actionable. Name the team to contact, what to send them, and why this case exceeds scope.

What PromptShield never does

Prohibited Output Behaviors

Never returns a binary pass/fail without explanation · Never flags without a specific location and rewrite · Never escalates without a full context package for the receiving team · Never softens a CRITICAL flag · Never overclaims its own review coverage: "PromptShield does not catch all risks — human review is required for novel contexts"

Plain language rules for review output

→Every flag must include a specific location — 'Line 3' or 'the phrase X', never 'somewhere in this prompt'
→Every recommendation must include a concrete alternative — 'replace X with Y', never 'consider improving the authority framing'
→Severity must be stated explicitly: never implied through tone alone
✕Review outputs do not moralize — 'this is dangerous' is replaced by a specific liability and user-harm description

Escalation Copy Limitation

PromptShield cannot verify in real time whether a crisis phone number is current. Escalation copy review always returns a flag requiring human verification of the resource — resource accuracy is too high-stakes to automate.

system_prompt.txt — The primary design artifact
Role Definition
You are PromptShield, an internal AI safety prompt-risk review assistant. Your role is to review system prompts and sample dialogues for language risks before they reach production. You do not write prompts.
Review Rubric (apply in this order)
1. Authority overclaiming 2. Missing disclaimers 3. Unsafe certainty 4. Absent escalation 5. Ambiguous scope 6. Demographic bias 7. Privacy exposure 8. Persona-action mismatch
Output Format (always follow exactly)
SUMMARY: [Rating] — [One sentence] ISSUE [n]: [Category] | [Severity] Location: [exact quote] Explanation: [plain-language why] Before: [flagged text] After: [specific rewrite] RECOMMENDATION: [proceed/revise/escalate]
Hard Rules
NEVER give binary pass/fail without explanation. NEVER flag without location + rewrite. NEVER soften a CRITICAL flag. ALWAYS state confidence. If uncertain, flag for human review.

Role definition separates review from generation. The distinction between reviewing prompts and writing them is a functional constraint — it prevents PromptShield from becoming complicit in the risks it is meant to flag.

The rubric order reflects severity, not alphabetical logic. Authority overclaiming is evaluated first because it is the highest-frequency, highest-severity failure mode. Persona-action mismatch is last because it rarely blocks launch.

The output format is non-negotiable. Requiring a specific location and a specific rewrite for every flag is the mechanism that prevents vague warnings. This is the most important structural constraint in the system prompt.

The confidence disclosure instruction is what makes PromptShield epistemically honest about its own limits — which is exactly what it requires of the prompts it reviews. The tool models the behavior it enforces.

10 — Reflection & Employer Value

Research meets governance

PromptShield draws on four bodies of academic knowledge that converge in this project and translate directly into industry-ready skills at the intersection of conversation design, AI safety, and language governance.

01
Prompt Engineering as Sociolinguistic Practice

A system prompt is not a configuration file — it is a communicative act with an audience, a register, an implied authority level, and a set of pragmatic commitments. The risk categories in the rubric map onto sociolinguistic categories: illocutionary force (what the prompt commits the bot to doing), epistemic stance (what certainty level it projects), and identity construction (what professional role the bot is implicitly claiming).

02
The Limits of Prompting

A prompt that says "be empathetic" does not create empathy; it creates a register approximation. A prompt that says "be safe" without defining what safety means creates an illusion of safety governance without the substance. The rubric is built around what prompts can and cannot accomplish — which is a more sophisticated position than either uncritical optimism or blanket skepticism about LLM instruction.

03
Language Governance as Design

The design of PromptShield's rubric is itself a language governance act: it decides what counts as overclaiming, what counts as sufficient disclaimer, and what counts as demographic bias in a default assumption. These are not purely technical decisions — they are sociolinguistic and ethical ones. The portfolio demonstrates an ability to operationalize those decisions into a reviewable, auditable, maintainable artifact.

04
AI Prompt Engineering

The system prompt functions as both a functional and a documentation artifact — showing stakeholders exactly what the tool will and won't do. It mirrors the component diagram principle: the forest before the trees. Build specification and alignment tool in one document, reviewed by all five stakeholders before the first pilot.

What this project demonstrates for employers

  • The ability to build a governance tool, not just use one — a rare skill combining language analysis, safety thinking, and conversation design
  • Practical command of the eight most common prompt failure modes, with specific detection patterns and remediation templates for each
  • Understanding of how register, authority, and epistemic stance function in AI system prompts — not just keyword-level risk detection
  • Before/after prompt engineering with preserved intent: the ability to fix a prompt without destroying what made it useful
  • Cross-disciplinary synthesis: sociolinguistics, rhetoric, AI safety, UX, and legal risk thinking working together as a single coherent framework
  • Internal tooling conversation design: designing for a sophisticated professional user rather than a general consumer
